This month, five redesigned IT degree concentrations go live at Central Piedmont: Cybersecurity, Cloud & Networking Technologies, Data Analytics, Software Engineering, and IT Support. We rebuilt all of them at the same time.

The safe way to revise programs is one at a time — one concentration per cycle, pilot it, learn, move to the next. Every curriculum handbook says so. We didn't do that, and it wasn't recklessness. It was arithmetic.

## The arithmetic

A single concentration revision, done properly — advisory input, course approvals, catalog deadlines, faculty development — takes the better part of two years from first meeting to first student. Sequence five of those and the last concentration gets modernized sometime around 2033. That schedule assumes the thing forcing the revision holds still while you work through the queue.

It won't. The pressure on these programs is AI restructuring entry-level IT work — the helpdesk ticket that triages itself, the junior role that quietly doesn't get refilled. That pressure doesn't hit one concentration at a time. Revising incrementally means telling the students who enroll in concentration five next year that their curriculum answers a 2020 job market, and we know it, and they should enroll anyway.

## The real reason: the shared core

The stronger argument for doing all five together isn't speed. It's coherence.

Revise concentrations separately and each one drifts — its own intro sequence, its own assumptions about what every IT student needs, its own vocabulary. Five years later you have five small colleges that happen to share a hallway, and a student who wants to move from IT Support into Cybersecurity discovers the paths don't line up.

Rebuilding together forced the uncomfortable, valuable conversation exactly once: *what does every IT student need now, regardless of concentration?* We had to answer it in writing, with all five program voices in the room. That shared foundation matters more every year, precisely because the entry-level jobs are un-bundling — the student who can move laterally is the student who stays employed.

## The unglamorous part

Nobody blogs about degree information sheets. I'm going to.

For each concentration we produced the boring artifacts: an information sheet, an advisor guide, student-facing materials. Not as an afterthought — as deliverables with the same status as the courses.

Here's why. A curriculum is not what the catalog says; it's what the advisor tells a nervous 19-year-old in a fifteen-minute appointment. If the advisor guide is wrong or missing, your redesign functionally doesn't exist. **A degree is a product, and products ship with documentation.** The courses are the engine. The advisor guide is the owner's manual. Nobody drives an engine.

## What broke

Honesty section. Three things:

- **Paperwork amplitude.** Five simultaneous packets through governance is not five times the work of one — it's worse, because every reviewer question about one packet raises the same question about the other four. Batch your answers or drown.
- **Prerequisite chains crossing concentrations.** Change a shared course's number or sequence and you've silently rewired four other programs. We caught most of these. Not all. The ones we missed surfaced in advising, which is exactly where you don't want them surfacing.
- **Decision rights.** Mid-process, it was genuinely unclear who decided cross-concentration questions — five program voices, one shared core, no umpire. That gap is why our curriculum committee now has a written charter and a RACI model. We wrote down who decides *because we had lived through nobody deciding.*

## If you're facing your own backlog

Two Monday-morning questions. First, do the arithmetic out loud: multiply your per-program revision cycle by your queue length and say the finish year in a meeting. If the year embarrasses you, incrementalism is the reckless option, not the safe one. Second, before the first packet moves, write down who decides what — a one-page RACI beats a semester of polite deadlock.

The five concentrations are live in three weeks. Ask me in a year what the redesign got wrong. Something did. That's not a reason to have gone slower — the 2033 plan would have been wrong longer.
