Every college has an AI initiative now, and every initiative has a slide about "faculty resistance." The slide is usually wrong about what it's describing.

I coordinate faculty development under an NSF ATE grant. A year into that work, here's the finding I keep having to repeat: I have yet to meet faculty resistance that survived being paid.

## The arithmetic faculty are actually doing

Redesigning a course around AI is real work — somewhere between a serious unit revision and a new prep, depending on the course. Now run the ask from the faculty member's side of the desk: do that work for free, on top of a full teaching load, using tools that change monthly, under an academic-integrity policy that will hold *them* responsible when a student misuses the thing they were told to embrace.

Declining that offer isn't resistance. It's judgment. If a colleague accepted it eagerly, you'd worry about their workload estimation skills — which is not the trait you want in the person redesigning your courses.

## What "resistance" turned out to be, case by case

When I actually sat with the faculty who were labeled resistant, the label dissolved into three specific things:

- **Fear of looking incompetent in front of students.** Faculty are professional experts. AI makes them visibly novice, live, in the room where their authority is the working capital. Nobody says this in a survey. Everybody feels it.
- **No time, honestly reported.** See the arithmetic above.
- **Trained skepticism.** These are the same people who were told MOOCs would replace them, clickers would transform engagement, and VR was the future of the lab. Their skepticism isn't irrational — it's *pattern recognition*. The burden of proof sits with the initiative, and it should.

None of these are fixed by a keynote about embracing change.

## What actually moved courses

Four things, none of them clever:

**Pay for the time.** Stipends or release time — the form matters less than the existence. The redesign work happens exactly where the paid time is, and nowhere else. This is the whole ballgame, which is why it's first.

**Course-specific help, not generic training.** The workshop that changed nothing: "Introduction to Prompting for Educators." The session that changed courses: two hours with one instructor and *their* syllabus, finding the three places AI actually alters what their students need to learn. Generic training scales beautifully and accomplishes nothing. That trade is not worth it.

**Small cohorts.** A faculty member will say "I don't understand how this works" to four peers. They will not say it to forty, and they will especially not say it into a webinar chat. Design for the confession, because the confession is where learning starts.

**Permission to be bad at it for a semester.** We framed first runs as pilots — expected to wobble, documented honestly, revised. The alternative frame, where the redesigned course must outperform immediately, guarantees nobody redesigns anything. You cannot simultaneously demand innovation and punish variance.

## The anti-patterns

You can diagnose a doomed AI initiative from three artifacts: the **mandatory webinar** (produces attendance records, not changed courses), the **policy memo with no examples** (tells faculty what not to do while leaving what *to* do as an exercise), and the **license purchased before the use case** ("we have Copilot now" is a procurement announcement, not faculty development).

All three share a signature: they generate evidence of activity without funding any actual work. That's the same disease I've written about before under the name process theater. AI initiatives are unusually susceptible to it, because right now the *appearance* of an AI strategy has market value all by itself.

## The Monday-morning test

Look at your college's AI initiative and find the budget line for faculty time. If there isn't one, you don't have an initiative — you have a memo, and the faculty have already priced it accordingly.

They're not resisting the future. They're declining to donate it.
