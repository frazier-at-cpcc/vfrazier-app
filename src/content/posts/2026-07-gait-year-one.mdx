Generating AI Talent — GAIT, NSF ATE award #2500901 — started in July 2025. We're one year into three. Dr. Carl Arrington is the PI; I co-PI the faculty development, core AI curriculum redesign, and community outreach workstreams. This is the year-one audit, done in public, because I think more grant teams should.

Here's the thing about grant proposals: they're written in the **optimistic mood**. It's a verb tense where every activity starts on schedule, every stakeholder is available in the semester you need them, and procurement takes two weeks. Nobody lies. The genre just doesn't have a way to say "and then the catalog deadline will eat four months."

## What survived contact

The curriculum work. CSC 214 — Artificial Intelligence II — shipped, aligned to Microsoft's Azure AI-102 objectives, and published as an open educational resource so any college can take it without asking. That was the proposal's center of gravity and it held.

The ethics workshop overperformed. *Would You Pull the Lever?* was written into the proposal as a component of the curriculum. It's become a standalone thing with its own life — honors classrooms, conference rooms, corporate L&D rooms. When a deliverable escapes the grant that built it, that's the good failure mode.

## What got redesigned

**Faculty development.** The proposal imagined workshops. We ran workshops. Workshops produce attendance, not redesigned courses. What actually moved courses was paid, course-specific work in small cohorts — sitting with a faculty member and *their* syllabus, not a room and a slide deck. That switch is a whole post of its own, coming later this month.

**Outreach.** The proposal imagined events people would come to. The events that worked were the ones where people were already gathered — advisory boards, high school classrooms, employer meetings. We stopped renting rooms and started borrowing them.

Neither change required NSF's permission at the level we made it. That's worth knowing if you're proposal-writing: describe outcomes precisely and activities loosely, because the activities *will* change.

## What nobody warns you about

The grant clock and the curriculum clock disagree, structurally. An award that starts in July meets a college catalog that closed the previous winter and a state course-approval cycle with its own calendar. Year-one curriculum work lands in year-two catalogs no matter how fast you move. If your proposal's evaluation plan expects student outcomes in year one, you have promised something the calendar forbids.

Also: anything with a GPU in it takes longer to buy than the proposal assumed. Budget the time, not just the money.

## What I'd write differently

- **Fewer deliverables, named more precisely.** Ten crisp commitments beat twenty fuzzy ones. Every fuzzy deliverable becomes a negotiation with your own annual report.
- **Faculty time as the first budget line, not the remainder.** The work happens exactly where the paid time is. Nowhere else.
- **Evaluation questions before activities.** Same lesson the xAPI project taught me: write the queries before the schema. If you can't say what question an activity answers, it's not an activity — it's a vibe.

## If you're writing your first ATE proposal

Three things. Read your college's catalog and course-approval calendar *before* you write the timeline — the calendar is the real PI. Find a mentor through the ATE community; the program genuinely wants first-time institutions and there are people whose whole job is helping you in. And draft your year-one annual report the same week you draft the proposal. If you can't imagine what it will say, the proposal isn't done.

Year one is where a grant meets the institution. Year two is where it earns the money. Audit again next July.
