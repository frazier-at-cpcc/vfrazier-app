Fall term starts this week, and with it another run of CSC 214 — Artificial Intelligence II — one of the core courses in the associate-degree AI programming we've been building at Central Piedmont under NSF ATE award #2500901.

Every curriculum is a bet about what the job actually is. Most programs keep their bets implicit, which conveniently makes them impossible to lose. We'd rather state ours plainly enough to be wrong in public. Here are the three.

## Bet one: entry-level AI work is integration, not research

What we left out of a two-year AI curriculum: training models from scratch, and the proof-heavy math that supports it. What we put in: retrieval-augmented generation, evaluation, prompt operations, deployment, and maintenance.

The claim underneath: the associate-degree graduate is not going to invent an architecture. They're going to take models that exist, ground them in an organization's actual data, evaluate whether the output is safe to ship, watch the cost meter, and maintain the pipeline after the consultants leave. That's the job. It's the same shape as the rest of two-year IT education — we don't teach students to design routers either. We teach them to run networks.

The predictable objection is that this trains button-pushers. Two answers. The transfer pathway to four-year programs exists for students who want the theory, and we route them to it. And "operate the thing reliably in production" is not a lesser skill than "derive the thing" — anyone who believes otherwise has never been on call. Labs teach the verbs; production teaches the grammar. AI work is no exception.

## Bet two: employers hire against certs, so we aligned to one

CSC 214 is aligned to Microsoft's Azure AI-102 certification objectives.

I'll say the uncomfortable part myself: building a college course on a vendor cert's spine should bother you a little. It bothers me a little. Vendor certs optimize for the vendor's platform, and platforms churn.

Here's why we did it anyway. Employers screening entry-level candidates don't read transcripts — they read credential lists. A course that leaves students one exam away from a market-legible credential converts education into hiring signal, and for community college students that conversion rate *is* the product. AI-102 also gets more right than the genre usually does: grounding, responsible-AI checks, deployment. Where it's thin — evaluation beyond the happy path, cost arithmetic, and the judgment call of *when not to use AI at all* — we teach past it. The cert is the floor of the course, not the ceiling.

## Bet three: ethics is a lab, not a lecture

The ethics module is not a week of slides. It's the NSF-funded *Would You Pull the Lever?* workshop: students vote on scenarios, argue, and vote again. The votes shift. The shift is the learning.

The bet here is that a technician who has personally felt their own answer change under argument is better equipped than one who memorized a responsible-AI checklist — because the dilemmas they'll actually meet (the automation that quietly unstaffs a role, the model output nobody double-checks) won't arrive labeled as ethics questions. They'll arrive as tickets.

## It's an OER. Steal it.

Grant money built this course, so it belongs to everyone: CSC 214 is published as an [open educational resource](https://github.com/frazier-at-cpcc/csc214-oer). Fork it, adapt it, teach it, tell us what's wrong with it. The fastest way to find out whether NC's first associate-degree AI bets are right is to have more colleges placing them.

And if the bets are wrong? Then we revise — which is the subject of a longer post coming this fall, about what AI is doing to the entry-level job these curricula feed. The short version: the job isn't disappearing, but it isn't holding still either. Neither can the curriculum.
